How Much Car Insurance Do You Really Need?

Choosing car insurance is a balancing act. You want enough coverage to be fully protected in a serious accident, but you don't want to pay for more than you need. This guide will help you find that perfect balance.

Step 1: Understand Your State's Minimum Requirements

Every state (except New Hampshire) legally requires you to have at least a minimum amount of liability insurance. This is usually expressed in three numbers, like 25/50/25.

  • $25,000 for bodily injury liability for one person.
  • $50,000 for bodily injury liability for all people in one accident.
  • $25,000 for property damage liability for one accident.

Warning: The state minimum is almost NEVER enough. A new car costs more than $25,000, and serious medical bills can easily exceed $50,000. Relying only on the minimum is a huge financial risk.

Step 2: A Smarter Liability Coverage (The 100/300/100 Rule)

For most drivers, a much safer and highly recommended level of coverage is:

  • $100,000 in bodily injury liability per person.
  • $300,000 in bodily injury liability per accident.
  • $100,000 in property damage liability per accident.

This provides solid protection for the vast majority of accidents without breaking the bank.

Step 3: Do You Need to Protect Your Own Car?

Liability only covers the *other* driver. To protect your own car, you need these two coverages:

  • Collision Coverage: Pays to repair your car after an accident, no matter who is at fault.
  • Comprehensive Coverage: Pays for damage from non-accident events like theft, fire, hail, or hitting a deer.

Rule of thumb: If your car is worth more than $4,000 or if you can't afford to replace it out of pocket, you should have collision and comprehensive coverage.

Conclusion: Your Final Checklist

For a typical driver, a strong, well-rounded policy looks like this:

  1. Liability Coverage of 100/300/100.
  2. Collision and Comprehensive coverage (if your car is not very old).
  3. Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist Coverage (highly recommended).

Start with these numbers when you get quotes, and you'll be on the right path to being properly insured.
